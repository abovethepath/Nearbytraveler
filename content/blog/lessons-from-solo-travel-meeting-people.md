# I Traveled to 10 Cities Alone — Here's What I Learned About Meeting People

After ten solo trips to cities across the world, I started noticing patterns.

Some trips were full of connection — evenings that ran until 2am, friendships that lasted beyond the trip, moments I still think about years later. Others were lonely in a way that was hard to explain to people who hadn't experienced it.

The difference wasn't luck. It was a set of habits and approaches that I gradually figured out — and a tool that changed everything when I finally found it.

Here's what I learned.

## Lesson 1: Waiting Is the Enemy

The biggest mistake solo travelers make is passive waiting. Staying at the hostel hoping someone will talk to them. Going to the popular bar expecting connection to happen naturally. Joining a tour and making polite small talk that goes nowhere.

Connection on solo trips requires initiative. Not aggression — just the willingness to be the one who starts things.

The travelers who consistently meet people aren't more social than average. They're just more willing to go first.

## Lesson 2: The First Night Sets the Tone

How you spend your first evening in a new city matters more than any other night. If you go back to your room and watch Netflix, you establish a pattern of isolation that's hard to break.

If you go out — even when you don't feel like it, even when you're tired from travel — something almost always happens. You end up talking to someone at a bar. You find a neighborhood you love. You see something that makes the trip feel real.

Force yourself out on the first night. Every time.

## Lesson 3: Locals Are the Key

Other travelers are easy to meet. You have an instant shared context — you're both visitors, you're both in the same unfamiliar situation, conversation starts naturally.

But the trips that changed my perspective were the ones where I connected with locals. The people who showed me the city they actually lived in. The invitations to things that weren't in any guide. The perspective that only comes from someone who calls the place home.

Finding locals is harder than finding other travelers. It requires different tools and different approaches.

## Lesson 5: Vulnerability Creates Connection

The most meaningful conversations I had on solo trips started with honesty. Admitting I just arrived and didn't know anyone. Asking for a recommendation and genuinely following it. Sharing something real instead of keeping the conversation at surface level.

Travelers are primed for deeper conversation. They're away from their normal context, which makes them more open. If you bring authenticity, you'll usually get it back.

## Lesson 6: Go Back

One of the most reliable ways to meet people while traveling: go to the same place twice. The second visit, you're already a familiar face. The bartender remembers your order. The person who was sitting at the corner table yesterday notices you're back.

Relationships form through repetition. Even compressed into a three-day trip, two visits to the same place can create enough familiarity for genuine connection.

## Lesson 7: The Lonely Evenings Are Worth It

Some evenings will be lonely. This is true even with the best habits and the best tools. Solo travel means sometimes sitting with your own company in a way that feels uncomfortable.

Those evenings matter. They're the contrast that makes the connected ones so vivid. They're also where some of the best thinking happens — about where you are, what you want, who you are when no one's watching.

Don't try to eliminate the lonely evenings entirely. Just make sure they're not all of them.
